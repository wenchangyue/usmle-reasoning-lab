quick_answer: >-
  The suppressed process is **gluconeogenesis**. After days of little food, liver glycogen
  is already depleted, so blood glucose depends entirely on gluconeogenesis. Ethanol
  metabolism (alcohol dehydrogenase + aldehyde dehydrogenase) raises the **NADH/NAD⁺**
  ratio, which drives pyruvate → lactate and oxaloacetate → malate and stalls the glycerol
  entry step, draining the substrates gluconeogenesis needs. With its last glucose source
  blocked, blood sugar falls to a dangerous level — and the same redox shift drives ketones
  (**alcoholic ketoacidosis**). It is not "just starvation," and not glycogenolysis
  (glycogen was already empty).
faq:
  - q: Why isn't the answer glycogenolysis?
    a: >-
      Liver glycogen only defends blood glucose for about 12–24 hours of fasting. After
      several days without food it is already exhausted, so glycogenolysis is not being
      "suppressed" — there is simply nothing left. Alcohol's biochemical lesion falls on
      gluconeogenesis, not on glycogenolysis.
  - q: Why can't muscle glycogen raise the blood sugar?
    a: >-
      Muscle stores about 400 g of glycogen (four times the liver), but muscle lacks
      glucose-6-phosphatase. It can break glycogen down only to glucose-6-phosphate, which
      is charged and cannot leave the cell. Muscle glycogen fuels the muscle itself; it can
      help blood glucose only indirectly, by sending lactate and alanine to the liver for
      gluconeogenesis — the very step alcohol blocks.
  - q: How exactly does alcohol block gluconeogenesis?
    a: >-
      Alcohol dehydrogenase and aldehyde dehydrogenase each convert NAD⁺ to NADH, so the
      NADH/NAD⁺ ratio rises sharply. That reverses lactate → pyruvate (pyruvate is pushed
      back to lactate), pulls oxaloacetate to malate, and slows the NAD⁺-dependent glycerol
      entry step. All three gluconeogenic feedstocks are jammed at once.
  - q: Why does the patient also have ketones?
    a: >-
      The same NADH excess depletes oxaloacetate, so acetyl-CoA from fat oxidation cannot
      enter the TCA cycle and is shunted to ketones; high NADH favors β-hydroxybutyrate.
      Low glucose and high ketones are two faces of one redox shift — alcoholic ketoacidosis.
