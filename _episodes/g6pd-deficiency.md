## Transcript

Speakers: **Clara** (teacher) and **Yue** (host / student).

### The hook

**Clara:** A healthy nineteen-year-old eats a plate of fava beans at a family dinner. Three days later his urine is the color of cola, his eyes are yellow, and half his red cells are gone. One enzyme explains all of it. But here's the twist — the exam won't ask you to name the disease. It'll hand you the enzyme and ask what one molecule is for.

**Yue:** And that's where people who "know the disease" still lose the point.

**Clara:** Welcome to USMLE Reasoning Lab. I'm Clara. If you'd rather reason through medicine than memorize it, subscribe and learn with us — new high-yield breakdowns every week. Let's work this one.

### The case

**Clara:** Nineteen-year-old man, previously healthy, family from the eastern Mediterranean. Three days ago, at a family spring meal, he ate a big plate of fava beans.

**Yue:** So far, just dinner.

**Clara:** Just dinner, until the reaction. Two days of worsening tiredness, and then the urine turns dark.

### The findings

**Clara:** On exam he's pale, his sclerae are yellow, he has mild flank discomfort. Hemoglobin 7.8, down from normal a month ago. Indirect bilirubin up, LDH up, haptoglobin low. The urine is dark and dipstick-positive for blood — but under the microscope there are almost no red cells in it.

**Yue:** Dark urine, blood on the dipstick, but no red cells. That combination means —

**Clara:** — the "blood" is free hemoglobin. Red cells are bursting inside the vessels and spilling hemoglobin into the urine. That's intravascular hemolysis. And the smear seals it: bite cells, and Heinz bodies on a special stain.

### The question — commit

**Clara:** Testing shows he's deficient in the enzyme that converts glucose-6-phosphate to ribulose-5-phosphate. Here's the question, word for word: the molecule generated in that conversion is necessary for which of the following?

**Yue:** Read me the five.

**Clara:** A — ADP phosphorylation. B — fatty acid synthesis. C — glycogen storage. D — ketone body synthesis. E — protein degradation.

**Yue:** Pause it. Pick one. Five seconds.

### The answer

**Clara:** The answer is B — fatty acid synthesis.

**Yue:** And I bet a lot of people didn't pick it, because the whole story screamed something else.

**Clara:** It did. Let's earn the answer, then deal with the trap.

### Name the molecule: NADPH

**Clara:** First, name the molecule. The enzyme that turns glucose-6-phosphate toward ribulose-5-phosphate is glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase — G6PD. It's the first, rate-limiting step of the pentose phosphate pathway. And a dehydrogenase pulls hydrogens off its substrate and loads them onto a carrier. The carrier here is NADP-plus, which walks away as NADPH.

**Yue:** So the "substance generated" isn't the sugar. It's NADPH.

**Clara:** Exactly. Run the whole oxidative phase and you get two NADPH per glucose-6-phosphate, plus a molecule of carbon dioxide. No ATP. The question is really asking: what is NADPH for?

### NADPH's two jobs — build and protect

**Clara:** NADPH wears exactly two hats. Hat one: protect. NADPH regenerates reduced glutathione, and glutathione is what neutralizes hydrogen peroxide and other reactive oxygen. Hat two: build. NADPH is the reducing power for reductive biosynthesis — fatty acids, cholesterol, steroids.

**Yue:** The vignette is pure "hat one." Oxidant drug, red cells getting shredded — the answer should be glutathione.

**Clara:** That's the trap, and it's a good one. Look at the options again. Is "regenerate glutathione" there? Is "protect from oxidative stress" there? No. Neither hat-one answer is offered. So the question is testing hat two — and the only build-with-NADPH option on the list is fatty acid synthesis. Fatty acid synthase spends two NADPH for every two-carbon unit it adds.

**Yue:** So the clinical story tells you the molecule. The option list tells you which job they want.

**Clara:** That's the whole skill.

### Killing the distractors

**Clara:** Now the other four fall fast, because none of them runs on NADPH. A, ADP phosphorylation — that's making ATP, driven by the proton gradient and NADH, not NADPH. C, glycogen storage — that's UDP-glucose and glycogen synthase; no NADPH anywhere. D, ketone body synthesis — acetyl-CoA to HMG-CoA to acetoacetate; the one reduction step, to beta-hydroxybutyrate, uses NADH, not NADPH. E, protein degradation — the proteasome burns ATP.

**Yue:** So the filter is dead simple: "Which of these needs reducing power?"

**Clara:** One of them. Fatty acid synthesis.

### Where glucose goes

**Clara:** Step back and put this on the map, because it makes the trap obvious forever. Every glucose becomes glucose-6-phosphate, and from that one hub it branches five ways. Glycolysis for energy — ATP. Pentose phosphate pathway for reducing power — NADPH, plus ribose. Glycogenesis for short-term storage. Lipogenesis for long-term storage — turning surplus sugar into fat. And glycoconjugates — the structural sugars.

**Yue:** And two of those branches are secretly connected.

**Clara:** They are. The pentose phosphate pathway makes the NADPH that lipogenesis spends. Branch two funds branch four. That single line — NADPH from the pentose phosphate pathway into fatty acid synthesis — is the entire question, drawn as a picture.

### Back to the patient: why he's hemolyzing

**Clara:** Now hat one explains the patient — and let's actually explain each step, not just name them. Start with why NADPH matters here at all. A red blood cell has no mitochondria, so the pentose phosphate pathway is its only source of NADPH. And NADPH is the only thing that keeps glutathione reduced — glutathione being the cell's one shield that neutralizes oxidants. Take G6PD away, and that shield can't be recharged.

**Yue:** Okay, but here's what nobody explains: why fava beans? Everyone just says "fava bean disease." Why would a bean do this?

**Clara:** Because the bean isn't inert. Fava beans carry two redox-active compounds — divicine and isouramil — that cycle inside the cell and throw off reactive oxygen species, chemicals like hydrogen peroxide that tear electrons out of whatever they touch. In a normal cell, NADPH-charged glutathione mops them up. In this cell, there's nothing to mop them up.

**Yue:** So the bean isn't magic — it's just an oxidant delivery system.

**Clara:** That's the key, and it unlocks the whole trigger list. Primaquine, dapsone, sulfa drugs, nitrofurantoin, and — most common of all — infection: none of them is special. They all do the same thing the beans do — they raise oxidative stress. A G6PD-deficient cell fails at exactly one job, defending against oxidation, so anything that oxidizes it can tip it over. You don't memorize the list; you understand the one weakness.

**Yue:** So then the hemoglobin takes the hit.

**Clara:** Follow the chain. The un-mopped hydrogen peroxide oxidizes hemoglobin; oxidized hemoglobin denatures and clumps into Heinz bodies; the spleen's macrophages bite each clump out — that's your bite cells — and the damaged cells rupture; hemoglobin spills into the plasma and out into the urine. Cola urine, yellow eyes, hemoglobin of 7.8. Every finding in the stem is one link in that chain.

**Yue:** And it's an X-linked story — that's why it's a young man.

**Clara:** X-linked recessive, most obvious in males, most common in people of Mediterranean, African, and Asian descent — the malaria belt. And the take-home beats the list: this cell can't handle oxidative stress, so any oxidant — beans, drugs, or infection — sets off the same hemolysis.

### The trap, named

**Clara:** Here's the trap in one line. The stem spends five sentences building a disease so your brain reaches for the disease's mechanism — antioxidant defense. But the question asked what the molecule is for, and then only offered you the molecule's other job.

**Yue:** So the fix is to answer the question that's on the screen, not the one the vignette put in your head.

**Clara:** Read the options before you commit to a mechanism. The options are the exam telling you which hat it wants.

### What would change the answer

**Clara:** Two clean what-ifs. One: leave the vignette exactly as it is, but change one option to "regenerating reduced glutathione" — now that is the answer, because the clinical picture points straight at hat one. Same molecule, different job offered.

**Yue:** So the vignette never changed the molecule — only the option list changes the answer.

**Clara:** Two: keep the options, but make the deficient enzyme fructokinase or aldolase B — now you're not in the pentose phosphate pathway at all, you're in fructose metabolism, and NADPH never enters the story. The enzyme names the pathway; the pathway names the molecule; the options name the job.

### Take-home

**Clara:** One rule to carry out. NADPH has two jobs — build and protect. The pentose phosphate pathway makes it. When a question hands you NADPH, the answer is whichever of those two jobs the options actually offer — don't let the clinical story pick for you.

**Yue:** Molecule from the pathway. Job from the options.

**Clara:** That's the point, and it transfers to a dozen other questions.
